Humans Are Only So Much Theory

February 25, 2025 · 6 min read

I’ll be the first to admit, I often get very lost in the world of academia and academic literature.

Not because I find these articles and essays particularly challenging or difficult to read (though they most certainly are), but because I very frequently find myself feeling very detached from what it is I am reading. Especially when it comes to the social sciences. I’ll explain what I mean.

Currently, my entire college schedule consists purely of four social sciences and humanities courses: a course in anthropology, a course in economics, a course philosophy, and a course in English. During the semester, my actual course work consists mostly of reading other scholars’ work, being told to remember some philosopher’s notion about the way human beings are or a poet’s genius hidden within his words.

Now, don’t get me wrong, this is NOT an article critiquing the school system. I am not trying to be one of those people who says “school is useless, it’s just brainwashing for the youth.” Education is, in my opinion, the single most important thing for a person to acquire.

But, this is an article where I seek to reflect on the reflexivity of our higher-education’s fields of study. What do I mean by this?

Jason Blakely, a professor at Pepperdine University, published a book called “We Built Reality: How Social Science Infiltrated Culture, Politics, and Power.” Within this book, he outlines a concept within the social sciences that he calls the “double-hermeneutic effect,” or the “Double-H” effect for short. If you don’t know already, hermeneutics is itself the method or of interpretation. In a word, it is the art of how we understand things, from texts to communication to human beings.

So, when Blakely outlines his so-called “double-hermeneutic effect,” what he attempts to do in his book is describe the process by which our scholarly endeavor to perform hermeneutics to understand the world around us indirectly causes us to perform hermeneutics on ourselves. In other words, in interpreting the world around us and creating boundaries and guidelines by which we categorize the world, we are in a way performing an analysis on ourselves that reveals our values, beliefs, and ideas. This categorization, in turn, leads to what for all intents and purposes is a feedback loop. As Blakely describes, when we divide the world into categories, we change social behavior to reflect those categories, thereby reinforcing their existence.

That might have been a pretty exhausting blurb about this scholarly notion, so I’ll break it down into simpler terms. Let’s take the field of economics, for example. Within standard economic models, the basic assumption is that human beings will be selfish. Looking at things like supply and demand, in constructing our models, we set the assumption that producers and consumers will want to always maximize profit or benefit, and it is these maximizations that lead to predictions of how the world might turn out. Of course, these are very elementary assumptions, and I believe (I hope) that no economist can seriously sit down and say that the sole driving motivator of the economy is selfishness. But, nonetheless, they are very critical assumptions within the field.

From these assumptions, the economic ideology of capitalism was born. Within its core principles lies the assumption that private ownership and the ability to pursue profit are the sole drivers of societal innovation and prosperity. And now, you can probably see where I am going with this.

Given that the United States and most of the world runs under the banner of capitalism, its systems have directly impacted the way in which people see and interact with the world. Being raised under the ideology’s influence, many people freely accept the system’s tenets. And thus, a cycle is born. Our initial assumptions about the world led to the construction of a system built to accommodate said assumptions, which in turn caused those assumptions to become reality.

This is the essence of Blakely’s “Double-H” effect. Theory affects social reality, and social reality affects theory. We, whether we recognize it or not, directly influence the way our society works through the ways we categorize and assume things about it.

And this is where I want to reflect on my getting lost in the world of academia. In my college courses I am told that “these are the ways by which human beings operate.” I am told that the human being is a purely rational animal in economics. I am told that culture can be divided into rigid genres and concepts in anthropology. I am told of everything that the human being is, but truthfully, I never really feel like anything that I am told is truly who I am.

When I write, whether it be poetry or short articles like this one, I am not thinking of the 100 different references and metaphors that English analysts may ascribe to my work in post. When I say a proverb to my friend, I am not thinking of the witty and standardly traditional notions that anthropological folklorists say that proverbs must invoke. When I give my money to a local business, I am not thinking of the rationality or deep opportunity hidden costs of my action. Instead, I merely act, and I merely do.

And I’d like to believe that many other people operate exactly like me. We’re human beings after all, not robots. We act often before we think, even though the motto is always to “think before you act.”

Now this is not to say that human beings are this irrational creature that we can never understand. No, I totally believe that there are some aspects of humanity that we can understand from a scholarly lense.

But, what I am trying to say is that we need to be cautious to not box humanity and human behavior into these rigid genres and categories. We need to recognize the power that academia has over human behavior, and we need to make sure that our publishings and works do not strip the humanity from our fellow brothers and sisters. When we’re at the top, it is easy to look down and assume that there is some method to the madness that one can unlock after years and years of hard work. A “theory of everything,” one might say.

But I propose that we aren’t often creatures of habit or creatures of rationality. We don’t live by unbreakable principles or think in one specific way. We live often times through our feelings, and ultimately, there is inherently nothing wrong with that. Civilization was not built by sitting down at a table and trying to rationalize humans. It was built by doing.

And so, let’s all try not to constrain human behavior to the limits of principles and laws. Humans are only so much theory, and theory can only take us so far before it crumbles in the face of raw humanity.
