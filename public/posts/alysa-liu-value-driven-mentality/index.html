Alysa Liu and the Purely Value-Driven Mentality of Modern Sports

February 20, 2025 · 4 min read

If you haven’t heard already, Alysa Liu, the 20-year-old female figure skater from Oakland, California, recently won gold for Team USA at the 2026 Winter Olympics by scoring 226.79 points! Woooooo! USA, USA, USA!

But, wait a minute, if all that happened was that Alysa Liu won Team USA a gold medal at the Olympics and, at least for a brief moment, united our nation’s vast partisan differences through her winning of the first female figure skating gold medal in nearly 24 years for the United States, why the hell am I even writing an article about this? Isn’t this topic already oversaturated enough?

Yes, but my goal in this article is to approach Alysa Liu’s victory from a different angle.

A curious phenomenon that I have noticed hidden amongst what should be unanimous praise for Liu’s contributions to Team USA is a subsect of individuals who seem to be bent on critiquing and downplaying her achievements. Specifically, they like to cite this noticeable reality:

In the 2022 Winter Olympics, the gold medalist Anna Shcherbakova won her honors by scoring a decisive 255.95 points. Apart of the esteemed Russian Olympic Committee (ROC), her first place score, by obvious measure, surpasses Liu’s first place score by nearly 30 whole points. If you are familiar with the evolution of the realm of sports, an obvious comment that could arise about this differential should be apparent: “This modern generation of athletes is soft; women’s figure skating isn’t the same without the Russians.”

This notion that the field of women’s figure skating has devolved or has turned “Mickey” is in light of the global controversy surrounding the horrifying measures that the ROC subjected their athletes to. With forced doping and child abuse scandals floating around the 2022 Winter Olympics, the image of the “perfect Russian figure skater” has been confronted by the reality of the harshness and grooming that came with raising many of Russia’s top female figure skaters.

So, how does this all relate back to the idea of a purely value-driven mentality? Well, with Alysa Liu’s refreshing take on modern figure skating and her notable Gen-Z fame due to her seamless talent in blending talented artistry with personal joy for the sport, it seems as if many people, in trying to downplay her 2026 gold medal performances, are placing the value that athletes bring to a sport over all other metrics. Just as we see in modern politics where the below minimum-wage worker who is scraping by to make ends meet advocating for the billionaire’s legitimacy and the validity of trickle-down economics, the sports fans delegitimizing Liu’s achievements seem to merely want to fill the pockets of the metaphorical shareholder that is sports. More, more, more, no matter how high the cost, is becoming the new motto for the world of sports.

Caring solely about the quantitative measures that an athlete can put up on the scoreboard and ignoring the reality that Liu herself initially retired from figure skating at the age of 16 from being burnt out and traumatized through the ways the sport pushed her to conform beyond her personal identity, it honestly seems like a lot of modern sports fans are mirroring capitalism in a way.

It’s a value-driven mentality. It’s “I would rather allow the Russians to keep competing even if it costs the sanity and livelihood of their overworked athletes if it means they keep racking up more and more numbers.”

But sports should be human-centric. Players aren’t just objects by which we bet on or cheer for. They are genuine human beings with their own lives and own personhoods. Though seeing the limits of how far human talent and hard work can push an individual in a specific sport is exhilarating and inspiring, I don’t want a world where all an athlete is seen as being obligated to do is generate endless value and metrics. Sports doesn’t need to be all a person is good for.

We shouldn’t be justifying a totally new sports environment where the human cost is ignored in favor of the scoreboard. We shouldn’t be inhabiting this sports-capitalist mindset: where athletes are only cared about based on how much value they can generate. We’re destroying the humanity of competition.

I would trade a traumatized robotic athlete groomed to merely be a competitive asset for Alysa Liu’s genuine enjoyment for the sport she participates in any day. Because that’s what sports are all about.

And so, at the end of the day, by choosing her own path and returning to the ice on her own terms, Liu has won a victory for every athlete who refuses to be turned into metric alone. Her historic victory is only a stepping stone towards flipping a new page for the world of sports.

Nobody wins if winning gold comes at the expense of our athletes’ lives.
